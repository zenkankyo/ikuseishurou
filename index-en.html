Raising Concerns about the Ikusei-Shurō Program

Background and Urgency

This statement is issued by Kazuo Arai, President of the National Association of Supervisory Organizations (NASO), to sound the alarm over the serious impacts likely to result from the government’s proposed “Ikusei-Shurō Program,” and to call for its reconsideration.

As a replacement for the Technical Intern Training Program, the new proposal is strongly criticized for imposing harsh regulations, ignoring the realities of an aging society with labor shortages, and—above all—discouraging the very foreign workers who wish to come and work in Japan.

Harsh Regulations Embedded in the Proposal

The Ikusei-Shurō proposal contains alarming elements that could shake the very foundations of small and micro enterprises. We detail three key points below.

Issue 1: “Japanese-Language Qualification Requirement” That Drives Talent Away

What the proposal requires: Those who wish to work in Japan must, before employment, either pass Japanese language level A1 (roughly JLPT N5) or complete coursework at a certified Japanese-language education institution. To become a Specified Skilled Worker, even higher levels of Japanese certification are required.

Concerns on the ground: Why impose excessive effort, cost, and time on foreign nationals who already struggle to find work in their home countries? With the triple blow of a weak yen, rising prices, and heavier public burdens reducing their take-home pay in Japan, adding a difficult language qualification is a blunder tantamount to surrendering the competition for talent to neighboring countries.

　　

Breakdown of JLPT test-takers by background

As the chart shows, most JLPT examinees are “students,” “researchers,” or otherwise highly educated individuals. Even among these cohorts, the pass rate for N5 is around 50%, and for N4 it is around 40%.

(Sources for chart and figures: Japan Foundation; Japan Educational Exchanges and Services websites.)

Our position: The Japanese-language qualification requirement should be withdrawn. On the ground, motivation and practical ability matter more than language. We seek diligent workers who devote themselves to any task, not necessarily those with advanced language skills. There are many who work earnestly despite limited Japanese. Making certification a requirement will drive away precisely the conscientious, hardworking people we need.

Issue 2: Excessive Burdens that Devastate Small and Micro Businesses

  • Mandatory “same wages as Japanese”: For foreign workers who already possess a certain level of professional skill (e.g., Specified Skilled Workers), comparable pay is understandable. But is “the same as Japanese employees” appropriate for newcomers who have yet to learn the job and everything else?
  • Seniority-like pay increases based on time employed: This is reminiscent of a socialist rule. Has the long-abandoned seniority system been resurrected—but only for foreign workers?
  • Taking work hours away for qualifications training? We already carve out work time so trainees can study for skills tests. Now we are told to find an additional 100 hours for Japanese study—at company expense.
  • Costs that keep rising: Wages will rise. In some sending countries, even reasonable dorm fees cannot be charged. Under the labels of tuition, fees, and exam charges, money is being siphoned off to government-related bodies one after another. Membership fees for sectoral councils may also be added.

Estimated additional burden: roughly 1,000,000+ JPY per worker

(This likely increases further when factoring in reduced productivity from fewer work hours, higher social insurance contributions, and other expenses.)

Concerns on the ground: Even now, accepting just one trainee requires paperwork the size of a weekly magazine. After returning from factories, sites, and fields, we struggle with unfamiliar office tasks. Please reduce the paperwork. Do not add further obligations or administrative procedures.

Our position: People are the lifeblood of small and micro businesses. But without considering costs, we cannot invest endlessly in hiring. Without people, performance falls; without profits, there will be no successors.

Issue 3: Headcount Caps and Heavier Workloads That Cripple Supervisory Organizations

Current role: Supervisory organizations (cooperatives and chambers) have supported small and micro businesses by managing, educating, and resolving issues for foreign trainees, acting as a bridge to government and sending countries.

Government-led black-boxing: Under the Ikusei-Shurō Program, supervisory organizations would face strict caps on scale while their workloads expand.

Unrealistic supervision limits

  • Each staff member may supervise up to 8 companies
  • Each staff member may supervise up to 40 workers

Do this, and that, and the other…

  • Regular audits, ad-hoc audits, site guidance, periodic reports
  • Stricter reporting and contact with authorities
  • Support for worker job changes
  • Japanese-language education and skills support
  • Strict neutrality rather than siding with small businesses
  • Mandatory external auditors
  • Restrictions on staff eligible to conduct audits

Our position: We firmly oppose capping the number of companies and foreign nationals that can be supervised based on an organization’s headcount. Is there scientific evidence that limiting one staff member to 8 companies and 40 workers will dramatically improve management of foreign workers? Numerical caps are not only meaningless; they will clearly create “supervisory-orphaned” companies and workers who fall outside the cap.

Voices from the Field

Are you serious about making trainees obtain a Japanese-language qualification before entry? How exactly are we supposed to have them study for the exam? How will you administer tests for hundreds of thousands of examinees? Who pays? People who have the time, money, and aptitude to study a foreign language generally do not choose to come to Japan to do dirty or heavy labor. You clearly don’t understand realities. You will end up losing diligent, devoted workers.

- Rebar contractor

With stricter regulations, the number of trainees will certainly drop. The ones who will suffer most are Japanese workers, because they will have to do the jobs currently handled by trainees. Our lines of work are ones no one willingly wants to do. Young Japanese won’t even look at them. The number of people willing to take over will dwindle further. Japanese-language education, new obligations, more paperwork—are you trying to force us out of business?

- Civil engineering contractor

I have worked with a supervisory organization for over 20 years to train Filipino workers. We have had no absconders, no major accidents, and our company has grown. Recently, the organization told us, “When Ikusei-Shurō starts in two years, we may not be able to work with your company due to headcount limits.” I have no idea what to do. There are no problems; our company, the trainees, and the supervisory organization coexist well. Why would you break this apart?

- Interior finishing contractor

Let’s Make These Voices Heard

To stop this merciless legislation, only political action remains.
Please petition the Diet members elected from your local districts.

The fear we feel now is not the mere paranoia of small and micro businesses. One administrative scrivener notes that if we become so fixated on “protecting foreigners” and “procedures for acceptance” that we end up destroying companies, that’s putting the cart before the horse. A JICA report also states that if this law is enforced, Japan will likely become a country not chosen by foreign workers.
